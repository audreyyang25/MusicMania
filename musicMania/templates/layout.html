A Brief History of Classical Music

Classical music is generally divided into four musical periods: Baroque, Classical, Romantic, and Contemporary. The following sections will give a brief overview of each era.

Baroque Period (1600-1750)

The etymology of baroque likely comes from the portuguese term "barroco", which means oddly shaped pearl. In general, Baroque music can be classified as complex, grand, heavy, and serious. The Baroque Period is commonly split into 3 overlapping periods: early Baroque (1580-1650), middle Baroque (1630-1700), and late Baroque (1680-1750). During the early Baroque period, composers based their music on ancient Greek music, which mostly has a vocal melody, accompanied by an instrument. In the middle Baroque period, composers began creating vocal and instrumental pieces for larger groups of musicians - new forms of music like the cantata, oratorio, and opera emerged. In the late Baroque period, polyphonic music, which is music that has multiple independent lines performed simultaneously, became popular. The piano had not been popular during the baroque period - the harpsichord was used instead. In general, Baroque composers expanded the size, range, and complexity of instrumental performance.

Classical Period (1750-1820)

The Classical Period produced music that was light and elegant. In contrast to the heavy textures and the complexity of the Baroque style, classical mmusic emphasized simplicity, which meant lighter, clearer textures. As such, classical music was often homophonic, which means that the piece had one clear melody with one or more supporting lines, which make up the harmony. The harpsichord, which was commonly used in the Baroque Period, was replaced by the piano. This change was significant because it allowed the performer to control the volume at which each note is produced. As a result, dynamics and phrasing became an important part of the music. During the Classical Period, the Sonata form was created and the symphonny was developed.

Romantic Period (1800-1910)

The Romantic Period is generally characterized by the intensity of energy and passion in musical compositions. Composers during this time focused more on nature, national identity, and the supernatural. Nocturnes, etudes, and rhapsodies became more popular, in addition to traditional structures like sonatas. In addition, composers experimented with new musical conventions and formats, using a wider range of dynamics and pitch, as well as more liberty during performances.

Contemporary Period (1890 - present)

The Contemporary Period can be subdivided into the Modernist Period (1890 - 1975) and the Contemporary Period (1950 - present). The Modernist Period includes the Impressionist, Expressionist, and Neoclassical movements, which represent a transition from Romantic to Contemporary styles. Impressionism was an especially notable musical movement that focused on using more unique "colors" and tonal combinations; it is often associated with composers Debussy and Ravel. The Contemporary period is notable for its rejection of traditionally accepted composition techniques and styles, and its exploratory harmonies.
