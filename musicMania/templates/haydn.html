Franz Joseph Haydn (1732–1809), an Austrian composer widely regarded as the "Father of the Symphony" and the "Father of the String Quartet," played a pivotal role in the development of classical music during the late 18th century. Born in Rohrau, Austria, Haydn spent much of his career in the service of the Esterházy family, where he composed prolifically. His contributions to music are particularly evident in his symphonies and string quartets, where he introduced innovative structural elements, expanded the scope of orchestration, and laid the groundwork for the development of these forms. Haydn's compositions are marked by wit, lyricism, and a keen sense of formal innovation. Some of his famous works include the "Surprise Symphony" (Symphony No. 94), the "Emperor Quartet" (String Quartet Op. 76, No. 3), and the oratorio "The Creation." Haydn's impact on the Classical era and his influence on future generations of composers, including Mozart and Beethoven, solidify his legacy as one of the foundational figures in the history of Western classical music.
